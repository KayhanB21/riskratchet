# Sprawl & defect prediction — findings and hypotheses (P21, phase 2)

Status: **directional, not conclusive.** Snapshot dataset of **15 OSS repos**
(10 general libraries + a 5-repo pycaret-adjacent ML/data-science cohort),
generated by `bin/calibration/` (harness phase 2). No product scoring change
follows from this document; it records what the data say so far, the hypotheses
they support, and what would confirm or refute them. Numbers below are reproducible
from the SHA-pinned snapshots in `repos/<name>/repo.toml` (see §3).

> **Update (corpus grown 4 → 10 → 15 repos).** Two expansions, opposite effects.
> The **narrow** claim keeps strengthening: dropping the file-line sprawl term now
> helps in **12 of 15** repos (sign-test **p≈0.018**, now significant). The **broad**
> claim keeps falling apart: the overall score is *not* a consistent defect predictor.
> The single most-powered repo in the whole corpus — **networkx (n_buggy=64)** — is
> **significantly anti-predictive (AUC 0.39, z≈−3.0)**: the score reliably ranks buggy
> functions *below* clean ones there. Four of 15 baselines are at/below chance
> (networkx, pygments, jsonschema, feature-engine). Meanwhile the ML cohort also broke
> the *other* tidy claim: in mlxtend and pingouin the **sprawl component itself
> predicts well** (sprawl_auc 0.76–0.79). Net: the result is **heterogeneous**, which
> is itself the finding — a single global weight change is exactly what this does *not*
> license. See §4 (F1–F5), §6.2, §7. (A harness bug found mid-expansion — `uv run`
> picking the wrong virtualenv — is documented in §6.9; it only ever caused spurious
> skips, never corrupted a committed snapshot.)

## 1. The question

riskratchet scores each function's maintainability risk as a weighted blend of six
components (coverage gap, structural complexity, branch gap, churn, public surface,
**sprawl**). The P24 investigation
([`docs/sprawl-component-finding.md`](../../docs/sprawl-component-finding.md))
showed that `sprawl` is, in practice, almost entirely its **file-line term** — a
*file-level* property (how big is the file this function lives in) injected into a
*per-function* score — and is near-orthogonal to structural complexity (pooled
Spearman ≈ 0.07). P24 could not say whether that file-line term is a real signal or
a size confound (El Emam et al. 2001 [R3]), because it only had *inter-metric*
correlations, not *outcomes*.

This phase asks the outcome question directly, in the spirit of Abreu et al. 2024
[R1]: **does the score — and the sprawl component specifically — predict actual
defects?** And operationally: **if we drop or dampen the file-line sprawl term,
does defect prediction get better or worse?**

## 2. Method (summary)

Per repo, for a historical snapshot commit `S` (~365 days before HEAD):

1. **Score** every function at `S` with riskratchet under full coverage (the repo's
   own suite replayed under coverage). → population with a real score per function.
2. **Label** via SZZ (Śliwerski–Zimmermann–Zeller [R2]): mine bug-fix commits in
   `(S, HEAD]`, `git blame -w` their deleted lines at each fix's parent to the
   introducing function, and track that function back to `S` (exact id, else body
   fingerprint via the rename matcher). A function is **defect-implicated** if ≥1
   later fix traces to it.
3. **Evaluate**: for each sprawl *candidate* (baseline; drop the file-line term;
   shrink its share to 0.75/0.90 function-weight; raise the 500/1000 saturation
   band to 750/1500, 1000/2000, 1500/3000 — all **component recomputes**, not weight
   overrides), compute the **AUC** of the score against the defect label, where
   `AUC = P(score of a buggy fn > score of a clean fn) = U/(n_buggy·n_clean)`,
   derived from the Mann–Whitney `U` (`bin/calibration/predict.py`,
   `bin/calibration/stats.py`). We report `total_auc` (whole score) and
   `sprawl_auc` (the sprawl component alone), plus the MWU `z`.

Code: `bin/calibration/{defects,szz,fixes,predict,rescore,coverage_replay}.py`.
The headline lever — `total_auc(drop_file_line) − total_auc(baseline)` — is a crude
single-feature **ablation**: how much does the file-line term help or hurt the full
model's ability to rank buggy functions above clean ones.

library" and "harnessable defect corpus" overlap far less than hoped — itself evidence
for §6.2.

Five observations:

- **F1 — the overall score is *not* a consistent defect predictor; its direction is
  sample-dependent.** Look only at the five well-powered repos (`n_buggy` ≥ 10):
  sqlglot 0.77 (z=4.2) and click 0.65 (z=2.7) are solidly positive; rich/requests are
  weak-positive; and **networkx is 0.39 with z=−3.0 — significantly *backwards***. So
  the most data-rich repo in the corpus says the score ranks buggy functions *below*
  clean ones. Four of 15 baselines sit at/below chance (networkx 0.39, pygments 0.36,
  jsonschema 0.48, feature-engine 0.37). The tidy "0.61–0.77" band from the first four
  repos was a small-sample artifact; the true picture is a wide, sign-flipping spread.
- **F2 — sprawl alone is non-predictive *on average* but not everywhere.** Across most
  repos `sprawl_auc` hovers 0.41–0.64 (near chance, often below). But in the ML cohort
  it climbs: mlxtend 0.79, pingouin 0.76 (and flask 0.83 on n=2). So the earlier flat
  "sprawl is noise" is too strong — sprawl carries real signal in *some* domains and
  anti-signal in others. Heterogeneous, not uniformly null.
- **F3 — dropping the file-line term *helps* in 12 of 15 repos** (Δ ≥ 0). The sign
  test is now **p≈0.018 one-sided** — the narrow claim crossed into significance as the
  corpus grew (8/10 → 12/15). The three non-positives are requests (−0.016), pingouin
  (−0.011), and flask (−0.039, n=2). Both clearly-powered separators (click, sqlglot)
  favor dropping; networkx (the powered *negative* repo) also nudges up when the term
  is dropped. This is the one claim every expansion has reinforced.
- **F4 — anti-predictive repos are real and now well-powered.** pygments was the first
  (n=9, z=−1.4); **networkx makes it undeniable (n=64, z=−3.0, significant).** Two
  repos where the *whole tool* points the wrong way is not noise — it is a real ceiling
  on "this score generalizes." Dropping the file-line term does not rescue either
  (both stay below 0.5). See §6.2/§6.7 for why this is plausible (big well-tested
  modules score high but aren't buggy; coverage-now muddies the dominant components).
- **F5 — the corpus is heterogeneous, and that is the headline.** total_auc spans
  0.36→0.85 with both significant-positive (sqlglot z=4.2) and significant-negative
  (networkx z=−3.0) repos. A single global weight is being asked to fit populations
  that disagree on sign. This is precisely the regime where per-repo AUC deltas mislead
  and **only a pooled, repo-stratified model** (§6.6) can say anything defensible — and
  where shipping any weight change off this evidence would be unjustified.

## 5. Hypotheses

Stated falsifiably, to be confirmed or rejected as the corpus grows (§6).

- **H1 (primary).** *The file-line half of the sprawl component is, on balance, net
  noise for defect prediction.* Removing it does not reduce — and often slightly
  increases — the score's ability to rank defect-implicated functions above clean
  ones. **Now supported by 12 of 15 repos** (sign-test **p≈0.018** one-sided),
  including both significant separators. Strengthened by every expansion (4→10→15).
  **Refuted if**, across a still-larger corpus or a pooled model, `total_auc(drop)` is
  reliably *below* baseline — the only negatives so far are requests (−0.016), pingouin
  (−0.011), flask (n=2). *Caveat:* "helps on average" coexists with F5 heterogeneity;
  H1 is about the file-line term's marginal contribution, not a claim the score works.
- **H2.** *What little predictive value `sprawl` has comes from the function-length
  half, not the file-line half.* **Now partially challenged:** in the ML cohort sprawl
  is *not* near-chance — mlxtend 0.79, pingouin 0.76 — so sprawl (blend and all) does
  carry domain-specific signal there. The sub-claim that the *file-line* half is the
  weaker half still holds directionally (dropping it rarely hurts), but "sprawl is
  basically null" (an over-reading of H2) is refuted by the ML repos.
- **H3.** *The score's defect-prediction value comes mostly from coverage and
  structural complexity, not sprawl.* Testable by ablating each component the same
  way (not yet done — see §7).

Note the scope limit baked into all three: SZZ measures **defects**, not
**maintainability**. Sprawl is pitched as a maintainability signal. H1–H3 concern
*defect* prediction only; see §6.1.

## 6. What might go wrong — threats to validity

### 6.1 Construct: defects ≠ maintainability
SZZ labels functions that later needed a **bug-fix**. Sprawl claims to flag
**maintainability** risk. A function can be sprawly and hard to maintain yet not
generate logged bug-fixes in a 1-year window; conversely, tiny well-structured
functions can be defect-hotspots. So even a clean "file-line term is noise for
defects" result does **not** prove it is noise for maintainability — only that it
does not predict this particular, observable outcome. This is the single biggest
inferential gap; the document's claims must stay scoped to defect prediction.

### 6.2 External validity: we are measuring the wrong population
All 15 repos are **mature, heavily-reviewed, well-tested OSS libraries**.
riskratchet's stated target is *AI-assisted code in side projects* — the opposite
end of the quality and review spectrum. Findings here may not transfer to that
population at all. The corpus is **selected for the ability to run a fast, offline,
whole-package test suite under coverage**, which systematically favors small, stable,
dependency-light libraries and excludes apps, services, async/IO-heavy code (httpx was
dropped for exactly this), and anything needing a live service. The enabled set is a
convenience sample, not a representative one.

The **pycaret-adjacent ML cohort** (networkx, mlxtend, pingouin, category-encoders,
feature-engine) was added specifically to push *away* from pristine utility libraries
toward messier, dependency-heavy, more application-like data code — a partial answer to
this critique. Two things came of it. (1) It surfaced the corpus's most important
counter-signal: networkx, a large graph library, is significantly anti-predictive
(F4). (2) It showed how *little* "ML library" overlaps with "harnessable corpus" — half
the candidates were dormant or un-runnable (§3). But these are still polished OSS
projects with green CI; they are **not** the AI-assisted side-project code riskratchet
targets. The external-validity gap is narrowed, not closed, and cannot be closed by
adding more PyPI packages — only by a corpus drawn from the actual target population.

### 6.3 Selection / survivorship bias in the label
Functions are only labelled if they exist at `S` **and** can be tracked forward to a
fix. Functions added after `S` are unscored; functions refactored beyond the rename
threshold between `S` and a fix are dropped (sqlglot: 79 such — a large fraction of
its implicated set). The functions that survive a year unrefactored and still get
matched are a biased subset — plausibly the *stable* ones, which is precisely the
opposite of where sprawl-driven churn would show up. This could suppress any real
sprawl signal.

### 6.4 SZZ precision and recall
- *Fix detection* is keyword-heuristic (`fix|bug|close|resolve|hotfix`): false
  positives ("fix typo in docstring") and false negatives (fixes phrased without
  keywords). `n_fixes_blamed` (9–40 per repo) is small.
- *Blame over-attribution*: `git blame` returns the commit that **last touched** a
  line, which SZZ treats as "introduced the bug" — a known over-approximation;
  `-w` and ignore-revs reduce but do not remove refactor/format noise.
- Merge commits are excluded (no single old side), a recall hit.

### 6.5 Statistical power
n_buggy is **1–64 per repo** (only five repos reach ≥10; networkx alone has 64, more
than the next two combined). At n_buggy=1–5 (feature-engine, flask, arrow,
category-encoders, pingouin, jsonschema) a single function's score swings the AUC by
tenths — flask's eye-catching 0.83 rests on **two** buggy functions and is
meaningless. The large Δ(drop−base) values (arrow +0.113, jsonschema +0.070) come from
exactly these low-power repos and should be discounted. What carries weight: (1) the
*sign* consistency across repos (12/15, p≈0.018), and (2) the handful of genuinely
powered repos — and those now **disagree on the score's direction** (sqlglot z=+4.2
vs networkx z=−3.0), which is the F5 point. Growing the corpus added one very valuable
high-power repo (networkx) and a lot of low-power breadth; neither substitutes for the
pooled, repo-stratified model in §6.6 — if anything the new heterogeneity makes that
model *more* necessary, not less.

### 6.6 The ablation is crude
`total_auc(drop_file_line) − total_auc(baseline)` is a single-feature ablation on a
fixed 6-component blend. It does not control for interactions, is not a proper
model-comparison (no cross-validation, no regression with the feature in/out), and
AUC deltas are not additive across components. A defensible weight decision needs a
real model (e.g. logistic regression with/without the term, pooled, cross-validated)
— see §7.

### 6.7 Coverage is "now", not "at risk-time"
The snapshot is scored with coverage regenerated from the **current** test suite run
against the **historical** source. Coverage of `S`'s code by today's tests is not
the coverage that existed when the bug was introduced. This biases the
coverage_gap / branch_gap components (which dominate `total_auc`, F1/H3) more than
sprawl, but it muddies the whole total.

### 6.8 Reproducibility drift
Snapshots are SHA-pinned, but `head_sha` (the end of the fix window) is **not** — a
re-run mines fixes merged since, so defect counts and AUCs drift upward over time.
The committed JSON is a point-in-time snapshot, not a stable target. Pin `head_sha`
too before treating any number as fixed.

### 6.9 A harness bug found mid-expansion (now fixed)
The ML expansion exposed a real bug in `coverage_replay._run_suite`: the per-revision
worktree lives **inside** the riskratchet repo (`data/calibration/_cache/…`), so a bare
`uv run --python <venv>` walked up the tree, discovered the *outer* riskratchet project,
and ran the repo's suite in riskratchet's own `.venv` — which lacks the repo's deps,
yielding spurious `ModuleNotFoundError`s and an empty coverage → a silent skip. Fixed by
passing `--no-project` so uv pins the given worktree venv. **Impact on results: none in
the corrupting direction.** A wrong-venv run produces *empty* coverage and is *skipped*,
never scored — so every committed snapshot used the correct environment by construction.
The bug only ever *suppressed* repos (it is why the ML cohort initially looked
un-runnable), biasing the corpus toward repos where uv's project discovery happened to
pick the right venv. After the fix, several "failed" repos scored normally. Lesson for
any future numbers: a "skip" in this harness can be a tooling artifact, not a property
of the repo — check the meta sidecar's exit code before concluding a repo has no signal.

## 7. What I expect from adding more repos — and the pre-registered test

**Expectation (pre-registered so it can be wrong):** as the enabled corpus grows to
~10–15 repos, I expect (a) `sprawl_auc` to cluster near 0.5 (sprawl weakly
predictive at best), (b) `Δ(drop−base)` to be **small and mostly ≥ 0** — i.e. the
file-line term keeps looking like noise-to-slightly-harmful — and (c) `total_auc`
to stay in the 0.6–0.8 band, driven by coverage/complexity, not sprawl.

**Scorecard at 10 repos (was the pre-registration right?):**
- (a) **Partly.** `sprawl_auc` mostly sits near or below 0.5 (six of ten ≤ 0.56), but
  three repos run high (rich 0.64, werkzeug 0.64, flask 0.83) — the last two on n≤3.
  "Clusters near 0.5" is defensible only after discounting the high-variance low-n
  repos; not a clean hit.
- (b) **Held.** Δ(drop−base) ≥ 0 in 8 of 10, magnitudes small except the two
  lowest-power repos. The core directional claim (H1) survived the expansion.
- (c) **Refuted.** `total_auc` did **not** stay in 0.6–0.8: pygments 0.36,
  jsonschema 0.48, arrow 0.50 fall at or below chance. The score's predictive value
  is far less universal than the 4-repo sample implied. I am leaving this miss on the
  record rather than rewriting the expectation around it — that is the point of
  pre-registering.

**Scorecard update at 15 repos (the ML expansion):** (a) **refuted further** — sprawl
is *not* uniformly near-0.5; it predicts well in the ML cohort (mlxtend 0.79, pingouin
0.76). (b) **strengthened** — Δ(drop) ≥ 0 now 12/15, p≈0.018. (c) **refuted harder** —
networkx adds a *significantly* anti-predictive, well-powered repo (0.39, z=−3.0). Net:
the directional H1 keeps surviving while the "score is a usable defect predictor"
premise keeps eroding. The honest summary is **heterogeneity** (F5), not a clean verdict.

**Decision rule (what would justify a 0.3.0 weight change):** *only* if a proper pooled
logistic-regression ablation (§6.6), **with repo as a random/stratifying effect to
absorb the F5 heterogeneity**, shows removing or shrinking the file-line term yields a
non-trivial, cross-validated improvement (or clearly no loss) — **and** the
maintainability-vs-defect construct gap (§6.1) is addressed or explicitly accepted.
The 12/15 sign result is suggestive but is *not* that model; with repos disagreeing on
the score's sign, a naive pooled AUC could be dominated by one big repo (networkx).
Absent the stratified model, keep the shipped weights and keep this a research note.

**What adding repos did *not* fix:** the construct gap (§6.1) and the external-validity
gap (§6.2). The ML cohort narrowed §6.2 but could not close it — these are still
polished, green-CI OSS packages, not AI-assisted side-project code. The higher-value
moves remain: (1) a corpus resembling the actual target (with a defect/revert signal),
and (2) the stratified ablation model — not repo #16.

**What broke across both expansions (actuals).** 4→10: all six general libraries
enabled with small recipe tweaks (pygments `wcag_contrast_ratio`; markdown `pyyaml` +
`--ignore` of a legacy `TestSuite`). 10→15 (ML, harder): of 10 ML candidates only 5
yielded a label — **dormant** ones score but have zero in-window fixes (patsy,
scikit-optimize, imbalanced-learn), and **multi-backend** suites under-collect without
the full DL/cloud stack (pyod, optuna). Survivors each needed a tweak: `setuptools`
(py3.12 distutils removal), `scikit-learn==1.6.1` (private-API import), `-o addopts=`
(stop `--maxfail` eating coverage), a src-layout correction, `mpmath`/`torch`. And a
real harness bug (§6.9) was masking several. Two compounding lessons: **easy-to-run ≠
informative** (networkx runs cleanly and is anti-predictive), and **informative ≠
easy-to-run** (the messiest, most target-like repos are the ones the harness can't
hold). That tension is the structural limit of this whole approach.
