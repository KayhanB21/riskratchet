## 1. The question

riskratchet scores each function's maintainability risk as a weighted blend of six
components (coverage gap, structural complexity, branch gap, churn, public surface,
**sprawl**). The P24 investigation
([`docs/sprawl-component-finding.md`](../../docs/sprawl-component-finding.md))
showed that `sprawl` is, in practice, almost entirely its **file-line term** — a
*file-level* property (how big is the file this function lives in) injected into a
*per-function* score — and is near-orthogonal to structural complexity (pooled
Spearman ≈ 0.07). P24 could not say whether that file-line term is a real signal or
a size confound (El Emam et al. 2001 [R3]), because it only had *inter-metric*
correlations, not *outcomes*.

This phase asks the outcome question directly, in the spirit of Abreu et al. 2024
[R1]: **does the score — and the sprawl component specifically — predict actual
defects?** And operationally: **if we drop or dampen the file-line sprawl term,
does defect prediction get better or worse?**

## 2. Method (summary)

Per repo, for a historical snapshot commit `S` (~365 days before HEAD):

1. **Score** every function at `S` with riskratchet under full coverage (the repo's
   own suite replayed under coverage). → population with a real score per function.
2. **Label** via SZZ (Śliwerski–Zimmermann–Zeller [R2]): mine bug-fix commits in
   `(S, HEAD]`, `git blame -w` their deleted lines at each fix's parent to the
   introducing function, and track that function back to `S` (exact id, else body
   fingerprint via the rename matcher). A function is **defect-implicated** if ≥1
   later fix traces to it.
3. **Evaluate**: for each sprawl *candidate* (baseline; drop the file-line term;
   shrink its share to 0.75/0.90 function-weight; raise the 500/1000 saturation
   band to 750/1500, 1000/2000, 1500/3000 — all **component recomputes**, not weight
   overrides), compute the **AUC** of the score against the defect label, where
   `AUC = P(score of a buggy fn > score of a clean fn) = U/(n_buggy·n_clean)`,
   derived from the Mann–Whitney `U` (`bin/calibration/predict.py`,
   `bin/calibration/stats.py`). We report `total_auc` (whole score) and
   `sprawl_auc` (the sprawl component alone), plus the MWU `z`.

Code: `bin/calibration/{defects,szz,fixes,predict,rescore,coverage_replay}.py`.
The headline lever — `total_auc(drop_file_line) − total_auc(baseline)` — is a crude
single-feature **ablation**: how much does the file-line term help or hurt the full
model's ability to rank buggy functions above clean ones.

## 3. Dataset

**34 repos** whose suites run under the replay budget and yield a defect snapshot
(general libraries + a pycaret-adjacent ML/data-science cohort). Each snapshot is
SHA-pinned (`repos/<name>/repo.toml`); raw per-function labels and AUC tables live in
`repos/<name>/defect-{labels,prediction}.json`. The full per-repo table — `n_buggy`,
`n_functions`, baseline/drop AUCs, `z`, and the Phase-B reproducibility flag — is
**generated** in [`defect-prediction-table.md`](defect-prediction-table.md); §4 below
reproduces the 16 well-powered rows (`n_buggy` ≥ 10). Span of the corpus:

- `n_buggy` ranges **1 → 224** (xarray); only 16 repos reach ≥10, only 5 reach ≥40.
- `n_functions` ranges 154 → 7827; the corpus is dominated by a few large repos
  (xarray, networkx) that also carry the most defect labels.
- A further **9 repos were tried and disabled**: dormant (zero in-window package
  fixes: patsy, scikit-optimize, imbalanced-learn, dateutil, emcee, featuretools,
  jinja2) or un-harnessable (multi-backend collection: pyod, optuna; CLDR build: babel;
  submodule data: tomlkit) — see each `repo.toml`. Tracked-but-disabled is the honest
  record of selection (§6.2/§6.3).

Each label file also records `n_implications_untracked` — implicated functions at a
fix's parent that could not be matched back to `S` (added after `S`, or refactored
past the rename threshold). Fast-churning repos lose the most (networkx, sqlglot,
markdown) — a direct hint of selection bias (§6.3).

**The ML cohort (§6.2 relevance).** A deliberate push toward repos that *resemble
riskratchet's target less badly* than utility libraries — sklearn-ecosystem, stats,
AutoML-adjacent code. The lesson of trying ~20 such candidates: roughly half are
**dormant** (zero in-window package fixes — patsy, scikit-optimize, imbalanced-learn,
featuretools) or **un-harnessable** (multi-backend collection: pyod, optuna). Every
survivor needed a bespoke recipe tweak (setuptools for the py3.12 distutils removal,
an sklearn version pin for a private-API import, `-o addopts=` to stop `--maxfail`/mypy
plugins eating coverage, a src-layout fix, `--no-project` + `pytest<9` from the harness
bugs in §6.9). "ML library" and "harnessable defect corpus" overlap far less than
hoped — itself evidence for §6.2. The survivors that *did* land are some of the most
informative repos in the corpus (networkx, xarray, lifelines, arviz, bayesian-opt).

Five observations (full corpus = 34 repos; this table = the 16 with `n_buggy` ≥ 10):

- **F1 — on the strongest evidence the score is mildly *anti*-predictive, not
  predictive.** Among the 16 well-powered repos, sort by size: the four largest after
  sqlglot — **xarray (n=224, 0.46, z=−2.0), networkx (n=64, 0.39, z=−3.0), croniter
  (n=55, 0.48), pint (n=31, 0.38, z=−2.6)** — are all at/below chance, three
  significantly *backwards*. Eight of 34 baselines fall below 0.5, concentrated in the
  biggest repos. There is still a solidly-positive cluster (sqlglot z=5.3, more-itertools
  z=3.6, bayesian-optimization z=3.1, click z=2.7, packaging z=2.8), but the
  data-weighted center of mass is now *below* chance. The original "0.61–0.77 everywhere"
  was a four-repo mirage.
- **F2 — sprawl is wildly heterogeneous, not uniformly null.** `sprawl_auc` spans
  **0.085 (boltons) to 0.88 (arviz)**. It predicts genuinely well in a cluster of repos
  (arviz 0.88, attrs 0.78, mlxtend 0.79, pingouin 0.76, croniter 0.72, bayesian-opt
  0.65) and is near/below chance in most others. "Sprawl is noise" is false as a blanket
  claim; "sprawl is a stable signal" is equally false. Its value is domain-dependent.
- **F3 — dropping the file-line term still helps on net: 25 of 34 repos** (Δ ≥ 0),
  sign-test **p≈0.0045**. This is the one claim every expansion (4→10→15→34) has
  reinforced. But the 34-repo set adds real counter-cases: **croniter (−0.123) and
  bayesian-optimization (−0.168)** lose a lot when the term is dropped — and both have
  high `sprawl_auc` (0.72, 0.65), i.e. repos where the file-line term is genuine signal.
  So "drop it" is the right *average* move and the wrong move for a meaningful minority.
- **F4 — anti-predictive repos are not noise; they scale with power.** pygments (n=9)
  → networkx (n=64) → **xarray (n=224)**: the larger and more data-rich the repo, the
  more clearly the *whole score* points the wrong way (xarray and pint both significant).
  Dropping the file-line term never rescues them. This is a hard ceiling on "the score
  generalizes," and it is strongest exactly where the evidence is strongest.
- **F5 — heterogeneity is the headline, and it is now statistically unmistakable.**
  The corpus contains significant-positive (sqlglot z=5.3) *and* significant-negative
  (networkx z=−3.0, pint z=−2.6, xarray z=−2.0) repos. No single global weight can fit
  populations that disagree on sign; per-repo AUC deltas are actively misleading here.
  Only a **pooled, repo-stratified model** (§6.6) can say anything defensible, and the
  most likely honest verdict it would return is "this static score does not generalize
  as a defect predictor" — which is a *stronger* reason to leave the shipped weights
  untouched than a weak-positive result would have been.

## 5. Hypotheses

Stated falsifiably, to be confirmed or rejected as the corpus grows (§6).

- **H1 (primary).** *The file-line half of the sprawl component is, on balance, net
  noise for defect prediction.* Removing it does not reduce — and often slightly
  increases — the score's ability to rank defect-implicated functions above clean
  ones. **Now supported by 25 of 34 repos** (sign-test **p≈0.0045** one-sided),
  including the significant separators. Strengthened by every expansion (4→10→15→34).
  **Refuted if** a pooled model shows `total_auc(drop)` reliably *below* baseline —
  and the 34-repo set surfaced the first strong counter-cases: croniter (−0.123) and
  bayesian-optimization (−0.168), both repos where sprawl genuinely predicts. *Caveat:*
  "helps on average" coexists with F5 heterogeneity; H1 is about the file-line term's
  marginal contribution, not a claim the score works (on the powered repos it does not).
- **H2.** *What little predictive value `sprawl` has comes from the function-length
  half, not the file-line half.* **Now partially challenged:** in the ML cohort sprawl
  is *not* near-chance — mlxtend 0.79, pingouin 0.76 — so sprawl (blend and all) does
  carry domain-specific signal there. The sub-claim that the *file-line* half is the
  weaker half still holds directionally (dropping it rarely hurts), but "sprawl is
  basically null" (an over-reading of H2) is refuted by the ML repos.
- **H3.** *The score's defect-prediction value comes mostly from coverage and
  structural complexity, not sprawl.* Testable by ablating each component the same
  way (not yet done — see §7).

Note the scope limit baked into all three: SZZ measures **defects**, not
**maintainability**. Sprawl is pitched as a maintainability signal. H1–H3 concern
*defect* prediction only; see §6.1.

## 6. What might go wrong — threats to validity

### 6.1 Construct: defects ≠ maintainability
SZZ labels functions that later needed a **bug-fix**. Sprawl claims to flag
**maintainability** risk. A function can be sprawly and hard to maintain yet not
generate logged bug-fixes in a 1-year window; conversely, tiny well-structured
functions can be defect-hotspots. So even a clean "file-line term is noise for
defects" result does **not** prove it is noise for maintainability — only that it
does not predict this particular, observable outcome. This is the single biggest
inferential gap; the document's claims must stay scoped to defect prediction.

### 6.2 External validity: we are measuring the wrong population
All 34 repos are **mature, heavily-reviewed, well-tested OSS libraries**.
riskratchet's stated target is *AI-assisted code in side projects* — the opposite
end of the quality and review spectrum. Findings here may not transfer to that
population at all. The corpus is **selected for the ability to run a fast, offline,
whole-package test suite under coverage**, which systematically favors small, stable,
dependency-light libraries and excludes apps, services, async/IO-heavy code (httpx was
dropped for exactly this), and anything needing a live service. The enabled set is a
convenience sample, not a representative one.

The **pycaret-adjacent ML cohort** (networkx, mlxtend, pingouin, category-encoders,
feature-engine) was added specifically to push *away* from pristine utility libraries
toward messier, dependency-heavy, more application-like data code — a partial answer to
this critique. Two things came of it. (1) It surfaced the corpus's most important
counter-signal: networkx, a large graph library, is significantly anti-predictive
(F4). (2) It showed how *little* "ML library" overlaps with "harnessable corpus" — half
the candidates were dormant or un-runnable (§3). But these are still polished OSS
projects with green CI; they are **not** the AI-assisted side-project code riskratchet
targets. The external-validity gap is narrowed, not closed, and cannot be closed by
adding more PyPI packages — only by a corpus drawn from the actual target population.

### 6.3 Selection / survivorship bias in the label
Functions are only labelled if they exist at `S` **and** can be tracked forward to a
fix. Functions added after `S` are unscored; functions refactored beyond the rename
threshold between `S` and a fix are dropped (sqlglot: 79 such — a large fraction of
its implicated set). The functions that survive a year unrefactored and still get
matched are a biased subset — plausibly the *stable* ones, which is precisely the
opposite of where sprawl-driven churn would show up. This could suppress any real
sprawl signal.

### 6.4 SZZ precision and recall
- *Fix detection* is keyword-heuristic (`fix|bug|close|resolve|hotfix`): false
  positives ("fix typo in docstring") and false negatives (fixes phrased without
  keywords). `n_fixes_blamed` (9–40 per repo) is small.
- *Blame over-attribution*: `git blame` returns the commit that **last touched** a
  line, which SZZ treats as "introduced the bug" — a known over-approximation;
  `-w` and ignore-revs reduce but do not remove refactor/format noise.
- Merge commits are excluded (no single old side), a recall hit.

### 6.5 Statistical power
n_buggy is **1–64 per repo** (only five repos reach ≥10; networkx alone has 64, more
than the next two combined). At n_buggy=1–5 (feature-engine, flask, arrow,
category-encoders, pingouin, jsonschema) a single function's score swings the AUC by
tenths — flask's eye-catching 0.83 rests on **two** buggy functions and is
meaningless. The large Δ(drop−base) values (arrow +0.113, jsonschema +0.070) come from
exactly these low-power repos and should be discounted. What carries weight: (1) the
*sign* consistency across repos (25/34, p≈0.0045), and (2) the handful of genuinely
powered repos — and those now **disagree on the score's direction** (sqlglot z=+4.2
vs networkx z=−3.0), which is the F5 point. Growing the corpus added one very valuable
high-power repo (networkx) and a lot of low-power breadth; neither substitutes for the
pooled, repo-stratified model in §6.6 — if anything the new heterogeneity makes that
model *more* necessary, not less.

### 6.6 The crude ablation, now replaced by a pooled model (shipped 0.2.10)
The §4 readout `total_auc(drop_file_line) − total_auc(baseline)` is a single-feature
ablation on a *fixed* 6-component blend: no cross-validation, no control for
interactions, AUC deltas not additive across components. It is suggestive, not a
decision. **0.2.10 ships the real model** the old text called for
(`bin/calibration/ablation.py`, `harness ablate` → `data/calibration/ablation.json`):
an L2 logistic regression with **one regularized intercept per repo** (absorbing the
F5 heterogeneity), validated **leave-one-repo-out**, with `sprawl` split into its
function-length and file-line halves so the file-line term gets its own coefficient
*controlling for* everything else. Within-repo AUC is intercept-invariant, so LORO is
valid; the 34-repo / 33,490-function / 812-buggy snapshot is `ablation.json`. Two
results:

- **The file-line term is confirmed net-noise.** Dropping it does not reduce — it
  slightly *raises* — pooled CV-AUC (mean 0.685 → 0.694; n_buggy-weighted 0.646 →
  0.658; Δ = −0.009 mean, full better in only **10/34** repos, **sign-test p = 0.024**
  in favour of dropping). Its standardized coefficient in the single pooled model is
  **0.039 with a 95% CI of [−0.094, 0.248] that spans zero** (repo-clustered bootstrap,
  1000 draws). This upgrades H1 from a sign-test on a fixed blend to a proper
  model-comparison — same direction, now defensible.
- **A nuance the descriptive AUC hid: the *ingredients* carry signal even though the
  shipped *blend* does not.** The fitted six-component model reaches ~0.69 mean
  within-repo CV-AUC — including on the repos where the hand-weighted score is
  *anti*-predictive (xarray 0.67, networkx 0.71, pint 0.58 under the model, vs 0.46 /
  0.39 / 0.38 for the shipped score in §4). So F1's "the score does not predict
  defects" is really "**riskratchet's fixed weighting blends informative components
  badly**," not "the components are uninformative." That is a stronger argument for
  *re-weighting work being worthwhile* — and a stronger argument for not shipping a
  hand-tweak now — than the descriptive study alone could make.

Caveats that still bound even this model: `l2` is fixed (not inner-CV-tuned); per-repo
`n_buggy` is small (the mean-over-repos AUC weights tiny repos equally with xarray);
and the model inherits §6.1 (defects ≠ maintainability) and §6.2 (mature OSS ≠ the
target population) unchanged — a better-fit model on the wrong construct/population is
still the wrong construct/population. See §7 for what it does and does not license.

### 6.7 Coverage is "now", not "at risk-time"
The snapshot is scored with coverage regenerated from the **current** test suite run
against the **historical** source. Coverage of `S`'s code by today's tests is not
the coverage that existed when the bug was introduced. This biases the
coverage_gap / branch_gap components (which dominate `total_auc`, F1/H3) more than
sprawl, but it muddies the whole total.

### 6.8 Reproducibility drift
Snapshots are SHA-pinned, but `head_sha` (the end of the fix window) is **not** — a
re-run mines fixes merged since, so defect counts and AUCs drift upward over time.
The committed JSON is a point-in-time snapshot, not a stable target. Pin `head_sha`
too before treating any number as fixed.

### 6.9 A harness bug found mid-expansion (now fixed)
The ML expansion exposed a real bug in `coverage_replay._run_suite`: the per-revision
worktree lives **inside** the riskratchet repo (`data/calibration/_cache/…`), so a bare
`uv run --python <venv>` walked up the tree, discovered the *outer* riskratchet project,
and ran the repo's suite in riskratchet's own `.venv` — which lacks the repo's deps,
yielding spurious `ModuleNotFoundError`s and an empty coverage → a silent skip. Fixed by
passing `--no-project` so uv pins the given worktree venv. **Impact on results: none in
the corrupting direction.** A wrong-venv run produces *empty* coverage and is *skipped*,
never scored — so every committed snapshot used the correct environment by construction.
The bug only ever *suppressed* repos (it is why the ML cohort initially looked
un-runnable), biasing the corpus toward repos where uv's project discovery happened to
pick the right venv. After the fix, several "failed" repos scored normally. Lesson for
any future numbers: a "skip" in this harness can be a tooling artifact, not a property
of the repo — check the meta sidecar's exit code before concluding a repo has no signal.

A second bug surfaced at the same time: the install pinned nothing, so `pytest`
resolved to the latest **9.x**, which *removed* hook APIs (`py.path.local` args) that
the ~1-year-old suites still use — they died at plugin registration. Pinned to
`pytest<9` (coverage of source lines is pytest-version-independent, so this does not
shift scores). Like the first bug, it only caused spurious skips.

### 6.10 Reproducibility — every repo was run twice (Phase B)
Because the score depends on a *regenerated* coverage run, the numbers are only as
trustworthy as that run is deterministic. So every one of the 34 repos was scored
**twice** with independent fresh-coverage runs and the outputs byte-compared
(`bin/calibration/verify_repro.py`; results in `repro-verification.json`). Findings:

- **Labels reproduced on all 34** (SZZ is parse + blame + fingerprint — coverage-
  independent, as expected). The defect *label* set is solid.
- **Scores byte-identical on 26 of 34.** Including big suites (sqlglot, xarray) — most
  repos' coverage is fully deterministic.
- **8 repos have mildly flaky coverage** (run-to-run score drift): category-encoders
  22/157 functions (14% — unseeded sklearn `random_state`), pygments 6/935, cattrs
  2/239, and arviz/networkx/pint/formulaic/tenacity 1 function each (<0.2%). The drift
  is in a handful of functions' scores, **not** in the labels.
- **AUC is robust to the drift.** Re-running category-encoders (the worst) end-to-end
  twice gave an *identical* AUC — the drifting functions don't change rank relative to
  the few buggy ones. The one caveat: category-encoders' AUC differs from an earlier
  committed run by ~0.06, so for the **flaky low-n repos the individual AUC is
  ±0.05-ish, not exact**. Treat their per-repo numbers as low-confidence; the labels and
  the pooled direction are unaffected. (Snapshots are SHA-pinned; `head_sha` is not, so
  numbers still drift across *windows* over calendar time — §6.8 — which is separate
  from the within-window reproducibility verified here.)

## 7. What I expect from adding more repos — and the pre-registered test

**Expectation (pre-registered so it can be wrong):** as the enabled corpus grows to
~10–15 repos, I expect (a) `sprawl_auc` to cluster near 0.5 (sprawl weakly
predictive at best), (b) `Δ(drop−base)` to be **small and mostly ≥ 0** — i.e. the
file-line term keeps looking like noise-to-slightly-harmful — and (c) `total_auc`
to stay in the 0.6–0.8 band, driven by coverage/complexity, not sprawl.

**Scorecard at 10 repos (was the pre-registration right?):**
- (a) **Partly.** `sprawl_auc` mostly sits near or below 0.5 (six of ten ≤ 0.56), but
  three repos run high (rich 0.64, werkzeug 0.64, flask 0.83) — the last two on n≤3.
  "Clusters near 0.5" is defensible only after discounting the high-variance low-n
  repos; not a clean hit.
- (b) **Held.** Δ(drop−base) ≥ 0 in 8 of 10, magnitudes small except the two
  lowest-power repos. The core directional claim (H1) survived the expansion.
- (c) **Refuted.** `total_auc` did **not** stay in 0.6–0.8: pygments 0.36,
  jsonschema 0.48, arrow 0.50 fall at or below chance. The score's predictive value
  is far less universal than the 4-repo sample implied. I am leaving this miss on the
  record rather than rewriting the expectation around it — that is the point of
  pre-registering.

**Scorecard at 34 repos (final, twice-verified):** (a) **refuted** — sprawl spans
0.085→0.88; not near-0.5. (b) **strengthened** — Δ(drop) ≥ 0 now **25/34, p≈0.0045**.
(c) **refuted decisively** — the *four largest* well-powered repos after sqlglot are all
at/below chance, three significantly anti-predictive (xarray z=−2.0, networkx z=−3.0,
pint z=−2.6). Net: the directional H1 keeps surviving while the "score is a usable
defect predictor" premise has gone from eroding to *inverted on the strongest evidence*.
The honest summary is **heterogeneity with an anti-predictive center of mass** (F1/F5) —
which is a stronger argument for leaving the weights alone, not a weaker one.

**Decision rule (what would justify a 0.3.0 weight change) — the model has now run.**
The rule was: *only* if a proper pooled logistic-regression ablation (§6.6), **with repo
as a stratifying effect**, shows removing or shrinking the file-line term yields a
non-trivial cross-validated improvement (or clearly no loss) — **and** the
maintainability-vs-defect construct gap (§6.1) is addressed or explicitly accepted.

That model shipped in 0.2.10 (`ablation.json`). On the *file-line* half it returns a
clear "**clearly no loss**": dropping it does not reduce pooled LORO CV-AUC (Δ = −0.009,
sign-test p = 0.024 in favour of dropping) and its coefficient's 95% CI spans zero. So
the **first** clause of the rule is now **met** — the 0.3.0 candidate to drop or shrink
the file-line sprawl term is supported by a proper model, not just a sign test. The
**second** clause is **not**: the construct gap (§6.1) and external-validity gap (§6.2,
mature OSS ≠ the AI-side-project target) are untouched, and the model is fit on exactly
that wrong population. A better-fitting model does not close those gaps.

**Therefore the standing decision is unchanged for 0.2.x: ship no weight change.** What
*has* changed is that a 0.3.0 drop/shrink of the file-line term is now the
model-supported front-runner (record it as such), gated only on addressing or explicitly
accepting the construct/external-validity gaps — ideally against a corpus that resembles
the target population. The model also reframes the headline: the components carry
real signal (~0.69 fitted CV-AUC, §6.6), so the open work is *re-weighting on the right
population*, not abandoning the score.

**What adding repos did *not* fix:** the construct gap (§6.1) and the external-validity
gap (§6.2). The ML cohort narrowed §6.2 but could not close it — these are still
polished, green-CI OSS packages, not AI-assisted side-project code. The higher-value
moves remain: (1) a corpus resembling the actual target (with a defect/revert signal),
and (2) the stratified ablation model — not repo #35.

**What broke across both expansions (actuals).** 4→10: all six general libraries
enabled with small recipe tweaks (pygments `wcag_contrast_ratio`; markdown `pyyaml` +
`--ignore` of a legacy `TestSuite`). 10→15 (ML, harder): of 10 ML candidates only 5
yielded a label — **dormant** ones score but have zero in-window fixes (patsy,
scikit-optimize, imbalanced-learn), and **multi-backend** suites under-collect without
the full DL/cloud stack (pyod, optuna). Survivors each needed a tweak: `setuptools`
(py3.12 distutils removal), `scikit-learn==1.6.1` (private-API import), `-o addopts=`
(stop `--maxfail` eating coverage), a src-layout correction, `mpmath`/`torch`. And a
real harness bug (§6.9) was masking several. Two compounding lessons: **easy-to-run ≠
informative** (networkx runs cleanly and is anti-predictive), and **informative ≠
easy-to-run** (the messiest, most target-like repos are the ones the harness can't
hold). That tension is the structural limit of this whole approach.
